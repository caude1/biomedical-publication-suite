# Section and Artifact Guides

## Paragraph Flow

Use reverse outlining for substantial revisions:

1. State the section thesis.
2. State the role of each paragraph.
3. Check that each topic sentence supports the section thesis.
4. Check that each evidence sentence supports its topic sentence.
5. Remove or relocate paragraphs that do not map cleanly.

A strong paragraph usually has one message, a topic sentence, concrete evidence, and a transition by cause, contrast, consequence, refinement, or example.

## Title and Abstract

The title should identify the study object, design or population when useful, and main construct. Avoid claims of novelty or importance unless the journal style expects them and the evidence supports them.

Abstracts should follow journal requirements. If unknown, use:

- Importance or Background;
- Objective;
- Design, Setting, and Participants;
- Exposure or Intervention;
- Main Outcomes and Measures;
- Results;
- Conclusions and Relevance.

Keep abstract claims aligned with Results and figures. Do not include a claim in the abstract that is only speculative in the Discussion.

## Introduction

Build from:

1. clinical, biological, epidemiologic, or methodological importance;
2. what is known;
3. unresolved gap or tension;
4. study objective or hypothesis.

Avoid announcing that the study is important. Make the gap specific enough that the study question feels necessary.

## Methods

Methods should be reproducible, not persuasive. Include design, setting, population, inclusion/exclusion criteria, exposure/intervention, comparator, outcomes, covariates, missing data, models, sensitivity analyses, software, ethics/registration, and citation of data sources when supported.

For complex methods, first build a module or step map internally. Then write in the order the reader needs to reproduce the work.

## Results

Lead with the primary cohort and primary endpoint. Report estimates, confidence intervals, p-values, counts, and denominators when available. Keep interpretation limited; save most meaning-making for Discussion.

Subordinate secondary, exploratory, null, and sensitivity findings unless they are central to the claim.

## Discussion

The first paragraph should summarize what was studied, why it mattered, what was found, and what the findings mean within design limits.

Then develop:

- interpretation of primary findings;
- comparison with prior literature;
- clinical, mechanistic, methodological, or policy implications;
- strengths when they are concrete;
- limitations;
- concise conclusion.

Use subheaders only when they help readability.

## Limitations

Use specific, design-aware limitations. Include direction of bias when possible. Do not use boilerplate filler.

## Figures, Tables, Captions, and Legends

For each display, define:

- display question;
- population and denominator;
- variables and units;
- model and adjustment set;
- uncertainty interval type;
- visual encodings;
- suppression or sparse-cell rules;
- exact figure/table file or source data.

Titles orient; captions explain. Do not put caveats into titles when a caption or footnote is the right place.

## Cover Letters

Cover letters should state:

- manuscript title and article type;
- central question and main finding;
- why the journal's readers should care;
- fit with journal scope;
- required declarations only when supported.

Avoid hype, excessive novelty claims, and internal process notes.

## Reviewer Responses

Point-by-point responses should:

- thank the reviewer briefly;
- state the action taken or reasoned disagreement;
- quote or paraphrase the manuscript change;
- give location if known;
- avoid defensiveness;
- avoid overpromising new analyses.

When a concern cannot be resolved, explain the limitation plainly and add manuscript text if appropriate.
