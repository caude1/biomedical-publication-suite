# Article Types

Use this reference to avoid forcing a full original-research template onto every artifact. Target journal instructions override this taxonomy.

## Original Research

Expected sections usually include Abstract, Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion, Limitations, and Conclusion. Use full evidence and claim controls. Reporting-guideline routing is usually required.

## Brief Report or Research Letter

Use compressed structure. The abstract may be short or absent. Do not require long Introduction or Discussion sections. Keep one clear question, one compact methods paragraph, the key result, and a proportionate interpretation.

## Case Report or Case Series

Prioritize chronology only when it is clinically necessary. Protect privacy. Include patient consent or ethics language only when provided or required by journal instructions. Avoid claiming generalizable effects from one or a few cases.

## Systematic Review or Meta-Analysis

Use PRISMA logic. Distinguish search strategy, eligibility criteria, synthesis approach, certainty, heterogeneity, and publication bias. Do not write as though original patient-level findings were discovered by the authors unless the analysis supports it.

## Diagnostic or Prediction Model Report

Use STARD, TRIPOD, or TRIPOD-AI as appropriate. Emphasize discrimination, calibration, validation, reference standards, and transportability. Avoid treatment-effect language.

## Translational or Preclinical Manuscript

Separate model-system evidence from human clinical inference. Use mechanism and biological plausibility language, but do not imply patient benefit unless directly shown.

## Protocol, Methods, or Analysis Plan

Future-tense methods may be appropriate. Do not report results that do not exist. Focus on rationale, design, endpoints, sample size, analysis plan, data governance, and feasibility.

## Correspondence, Editorial, or Commentary

Argument structure matters more than IMRAD. Preserve citation discipline and avoid overstating the evidence base. Make opinion clearly distinguishable from data.

## Grant or Project Narrative

Use the narrative-audit framework when the goal is a clean scientific story for grant-like review. Keep final logic rather than timeline, but do not misrepresent exploratory status or preliminary evidence as completed validation.
